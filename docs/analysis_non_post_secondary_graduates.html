Employment outcomes distribution

The following tables provide the percentage of non-college graduates that were categorized in the various employment outcomes at 1-, 5-, and 10-years after graduating high school.

Among non-college completers, workforce participation patterns shift substantially across the three observation windows, reflecting the transition out of postsecondary attendance and into more settled labor market positions over time.

At year 1, the population is still actively sorting. More than one in five individuals (21.8%) are attending postsecondary at this point — a group that will, by definition, leave the non-college completer population if they ultimately earn a credential, but who appear here as non-completers as of the observation window. Regional employment is already the most common workforce outcome (19.3%), while a combined 52.5% are either not yet meaningfully employed or carry no Minnesota employment record at all, reflecting the unsettled nature of the early post-graduation period.

By year 5, postsecondary attendance has collapsed to 4.5%, indicating that most of this group has exited postsecondary without completing. Regional employment holds relatively stable at 25.7%, and statewide employment outside the region grows to 15.1%, suggesting some outward labor market mobility. However, the two non-employment categories together still account for 54.6% of the population — with “no Minnesota employment record” actually growing from 21.3% to 30.7%, a notable increase that may reflect out-of-state migration, informal employment, or disengagement from the formal labor market.

At year 10, the attending postsecondary category disappears entirely, as expected. Regional employment remains the single largest employment category at 24.7%, roughly consistent with its year 5 share, while statewide employment holds at 17.9%. The most striking feature of the year 10 distribution is that 38.6% carry no Minnesota employment record — the largest single category at this time point — suggesting that a substantial and growing share of non-college completers have either left the state or are not participating in the formal Minnesota labor market a decade after high school graduation. The not-meaningful employment share drops sharply from 23.9% to 18.7%, indicating that those who remain in Minnesota are increasingly achieving sustained employment, but the overall picture points to significant long-term labor market precarity or geographic departure among this subgroup.

For exploring the relationships we will do the following;

  • For categorical predictors (e.g., Race/Ethnicity, Economic status, CTE achievement, PSEO participation), use cross-tabs with Cramer’s V and chi-square.
  • For continuous predictors (e.g., ACT, MCA scores, total CTE courses, county-level variables), use ANOVA to compare means across the employment states.
  • Run these analyses separately for grad.year.1, grad.year.5, grad.year.10 to see how relationships shift over time.

Dependent variables

The dependent variables will be workforce participation outcomes at 1-, 5- and 10-years after high school. The workforce participation categories at each grad.year.x are;

  • Sustained WF - region: 1,000+ hours with a single employer in a calendar year located in the region
  • Sustained WF - MN: 1,000+ hours with a single employer in a calendar year located outside the region, but in Minnesota
  • Non-sustained WF: has a Minnesota employment record but did not reach the “sustained” threshold.
  • No MN emp record: does not have a Minnesota employment record and is not attending post-secondary.
  • Attending ps: is attending postsecondary and meets one of the following criteria;
    • does not have sustained workforce participation within Minnesota or the region, or
    • does not have a MN employment record.

Demographics

Summary

Among the six demographic variables examined, effect sizes vary meaningfully across the three observation windows, though all associations are statistically significant. The rankings below reflect a general assessment across all three time points, with particular attention to the consistency and magnitude of Cramer’s V values.

1. Special Education Status (V=0.171, 0.107, 0.093) is the strongest demographic predictor overall, showing the highest effect size at year 1 and maintaining meaningful associations through year 10. Its signature is not reduced regional employment but rather persistently elevated employment instability and reduced statewide labor market mobility.

2. Economic Status (V=0.137, 0.083, 0.091) ranks second, with a strong year 1 association driven largely by differential postsecondary attendance. By years 5 and 10 the association weakens considerably, with the most durable difference being modestly higher statewide employment among non-disadvantaged graduates and greater employment instability among disadvantaged graduates.

3. English Learner Status (V=0.140, 0.074, 0.070) ranks third, with a strong year 1 effect that similarly reflects lower postsecondary attendance. Its most distinctive and persistent feature across all three windows is elevated absence from Minnesota employment records, a pattern that sets it apart from the other demographic predictors.

4. Non-English Home Language (V=0.114, 0.068, 0.061) ranks fourth and closely mirrors the English learner pattern in both direction and character. Effect sizes are modestly smaller at every time point, suggesting that English learner status captures the relevant variation more precisely. Given the substantial overlap between these two variables, their independent contributions will require evaluation in multivariate models.

5. Gender (V=0.058, 0.087, 0.107) ranks fifth overall, though notably its effect size increases across the observation windows rather than declining — the only demographic variable to do so. Its growing association reflects a widening gap in sustained regional employment favoring males and an expanding share of females carrying no MN employment record by year 10.

6. Race/Ethnicity (V=0.069, 0.043, 0.054) shows the weakest and least consistent association across time points. While American Indian graduates show a persistently adverse profile and Asian/Pacific Islander graduates display a distinctive pattern of high MN record absence alongside relatively high statewide employment among those who remain, the overall effect size remains small at all three windows.

Across all demographic variables, effect sizes fall in the weak to modest range by conventional standards, indicating that demographic characteristics alone account for limited variation in workforce participation outcomes among non-college completers. The high school accomplishment and postsecondary variables examined in subsequent sections are expected to contribute more substantially to explaining outcome variation.


Gender

Gender shows a statistically significant but modest association with workforce participation outcomes at all three time points, with Cramer’s V increasing slightly across the observation windows (V=0.058 at year 1, V=0.087 at year 5, V=0.107 at year 10), indicating that the gender gap in outcomes grows somewhat more pronounced over time.

At year 1, differences are small. Males are modestly more likely to be in sustained regional employment (20.7% vs. 17.4%), while females are more likely to be in non-sustained workforce participation (33.9% vs. 29.3%). Rates of postsecondary attendance and no Minnesota employment record are similar across genders.

By year 5, the pattern sharpens. Males show a notably higher rate of sustained regional employment (28.5% vs. 21.8%), while females are more concentrated in non-sustained workforce participation (26.5% vs. 22.0%) and no Minnesota employment record (32.1% vs. 29.7%). The sustained MN employment category is similar across genders (14.4% female, 15.6% male).

At year 10, the regional employment gap persists — 27.4% of males are in sustained regional employment compared to 21.1% of females — and males also show higher rates of sustained MN employment (19.8% vs. 15.4%). The most notable difference at year 10 is in the no Minnesota employment record category, where 42.5% of females carry no MN record compared to 35.7% of males, a gap of nearly seven percentage points. Whether this reflects greater rates of out-of-state migration, labor market withdrawal, or informal employment among women in this population cannot be determined from these data alone.

Overall, gender is a consistent but modest predictor of workforce participation outcomes among non-college completers, with males more likely to achieve sustained regional employment and females more likely to be absent from Minnesota employment records by year 10.

RaceEthnicity

Race/ethnicity shows a statistically significant but weak association with workforce participation outcomes across all three time points, with Cramer’s V remaining low and relatively stable (V=0.069 at year 1, V=0.043 at year 5, V=0.054 at year 10). The overall pattern suggests that racial and ethnic differences in outcomes are modest in magnitude, though meaningfully varied in character across groups.

At year 1, the most notable differences involve the no Minnesota employment record category. American Indian graduates show the highest rate of no MN employment record (35.7%), followed closely by Asian/Pacific Islander (30.2%) and Hispanic (28.1%) graduates, compared to 18.9% among White graduates and only 14.6% among Black graduates. American Indian graduates also show the lowest rate of sustained regional employment (8.2%), well below the overall rate of 18.5%, and the lowest postsecondary attendance rate (14.0%), suggesting early and pronounced disengagement from both postsecondary and the formal labor market. Black graduates, by contrast, show comparatively higher postsecondary attendance (27.3%) and non-sustained workforce participation (33.4%) at year 1, suggesting a somewhat different transitional profile.

By year 5, the patterns shift as postsecondary attendance declines across all groups. White and Hispanic graduates converge near the overall sustained regional employment rate (26.4% and 25.5%, respectively), while American Indian (17.0%), Black (17.1%), and Asian/Pacific Islander (20.6%) graduates fall below it. The no MN employment record category remains elevated for American Indian (38.9%) and Asian/Pacific Islander (37.4%) graduates relative to White graduates (29.7%), a gap that may reflect differential rates of out-of-state migration or labor market withdrawal. Hispanic graduates show a notably lower rate of statewide employment outside the region (11.0%) compared to White graduates (15.9%), potentially reflecting stronger regional labor market attachment.

At year 10, White graduates show the highest sustained regional employment rate (25.5%), while Asian/Pacific Islander (14.9%) and Black (16.1%) graduates are the least likely to be in sustained regional employment. Asian/Pacific Islander graduates show the highest rate of no MN employment record at year 10 (46.7%), followed by American Indian (43.3%) and Hispanic (42.9%) graduates — all substantially above the White rate of 37.7%. A notable exception is Asian/Pacific Islander graduates’ sustained MN employment rate (21.9%), the highest of any group at year 10, suggesting that those who remain in Minnesota are disproportionately employed outside the region, consistent with patterns of urban concentration. American Indian graduates show the lowest sustained MN employment rate at year 10 (4.7%), and the highest non-sustained workforce participation (29.9%), indicating persistent labor market instability within this group.

Overall, race/ethnicity is a weak predictor of workforce participation outcomes in this population, but American Indian graduates consistently show the most adverse profile across all three time points, and Asian/Pacific Islander graduates display a distinctive pattern of high out-of-state or non-regional presence alongside relatively high statewide employment among those who remain.

Overall, county unemployment rate shows a consistent and interpretable pattern: graduates who remain in sustained regional employment tend to come from lower-unemployment counties, while those who achieve sustained statewide employment outside the region disproportionately originate from higher-unemployment counties, a gap that widens over time. High school accomplishments

Summary

Among the high school accomplishments variables examined, effect sizes span a wider range than the demographic or high school characteristics groups, and the variables divide clearly into two clusters that mirror the two-pathway structure identified in the full population analysis — academic pathway variables associated with statewide employment and CTE pathway variables associated with regional employment.

1. ACT Participation (V=0.302, 0.155, 0.108) is the strongest predictor in this group and the strongest single variable examined in the entire bivariate analysis thus far. Its dominant year 1 effect is driven by a nearly 24-percentage- point postsecondary attendance gap, but its most durable signature across all three time points is substantially higher sustained statewide employment and lower sustained regional employment among ACT takers — the clearest expression of the academic pathway among non-college completers.

2. Total CTE Courses Taken produces the highest F-statistics of any continuous variable in the group (F=146.8, 101.3, 62.1) and shows the strongest and most consistent advantage for sustained regional employment of any variable examined. Its mirror-image relationship to ACT participation is striking: where ACT predicts statewide mobility, CTE course volume predicts regional attachment, with the no MN employment record group consistently showing the lowest CTE engagement at every time point.

3. AP Exam Participation (V=0.149, 0.101, 0.065) ranks third, showing a pattern nearly identical in character to ACT participation — high postsecondary attendance early, lower regional employment, and persistently higher statewide employment — but with smaller effect sizes throughout and greater attenuation by year 10. AP participants represent a small share of this non-completer population, which limits the practical weight of this variable relative to ACT participation.

4. MCA Reading Score (F=144.3, 42.0, 28.0) is the strongest of the three MCA measures, consistent with its role as the primary academic achievement indicator in the full population multivariate models. Its gradient is clear and durable: higher scores are associated with statewide employment and — increasingly over time — absence from Minnesota employment records, while regional employment and non-sustained work occupy the lowest achievement tier at every time point.

5. MCA Science Score (F=84.4, 38.1, 25.1) closely replicates the MCA Reading pattern across all three time points, including the three-tier consolidation at year 10. As in the full population analysis, its voluntary and self- selected nature limits its utility in multivariate models, but its bivariate associations confirm the broader academic achievement gradient.

6. MCA Math Score (F=89.4, 36.0, 34.6) produces a pattern consistent with MCA Reading and Science but with somewhat lower F-statistics at years 1 and 5. Its year 10 F-statistic (34.6) exceeds MCA Reading (28.0), suggesting that math achievement may carry slightly more differentiation at the later time point, though the substantive pattern is identical across all three MCA measures.

7. CTE Achievement (V=0.094, 0.106, 0.115) is the strongest categorical CTE variable and shows a growing association over time — the only accomplishments variable whose effect size increases at every time point. Its signature is the most distinctive in the group: CTE concentrators and completers show progressively higher sustained regional employment and lower no MN employment record rates relative to the no CTE group, with a 15- percentage-point regional employment gap emerging by year 10.

8. PSEO Participation (V=0.186, 0.072, 0.082) ranks eighth, with a strong year 1 association driven almost entirely by an 18-percentage-point postsecondary attendance gap. Once postsecondary attendance equalizes, its most durable finding is a persistent tendency toward statewide rather than regional employment, consistent with the academic pathway, but its effect sizes at years 5 and 10 are among the smallest in this group.

Across the high school accomplishments group, two conclusions stand out. First, the academic-CTE divide is fully present even within the non-college completer population: academic indicators predict statewide labor market mobility while CTE indicators predict regional attachment, regardless of whether a credential was earned. Second, CTE course volume and ACT participation are the two strongest individual predictors in this group, and their opposing signatures make them the most analytically important variables for distinguishing workforce participation trajectories among non-college completers.

PSEO participation shows a statistically significant association with workforce participation outcomes at all three time points, with Cramer’s V declining sharply from year 1 (V=0.186) to year 5 (V=0.072) and recovering modestly at year 10 (V=0.082). As with several demographic variables, the strong year 1 association is substantially driven by differential postsecondary attendance, which is expected given that PSEO participants have already demonstrated postsecondary engagement before graduating high school. It is worth noting that this analysis is restricted to 2011 cohorts and later, where PSEO data are available, which reduces sample sizes relative to other variables.

At year 1, the dominant finding is that PSEO participants are far more likely to still be attending postsecondary (34.8% vs. 16.6%), a gap of 18 percentage points that is the primary driver of the year 1 effect size. Among those not attending postsecondary, PSEO participants show lower sustained regional employment (16.3% vs. 21.0%) and notably lower no MN employment record rates (14.3% vs. 23.4%), suggesting that PSEO participants who do not complete postsecondary are more likely to be engaged in the labor market in some form — even if not sustained regionally — than non-PSEO graduates who similarly lack a MN employment record. Non-sustained workforce participation is also somewhat lower among PSEO participants (28.3% vs. 32.1%).

By year 5, postsecondary attendance has declined for both groups but remains notably higher among PSEO participants (5.7% vs. 3.3%), consistent with longer postsecondary enrollment spells. The most distinctive shift is in sustained statewide employment, where PSEO participants show a higher rate (18.9% vs. 14.6%), while sustained regional employment is modestly lower (23.4% vs. 26.2%). This pattern — higher statewide and lower regional employment among PSEO participants — is consistent with the broader academic pathway finding that postsecondary-oriented graduates tend toward employment outside the region. No MN employment record rates are similar across groups at year 5 (29.5% vs. 31.8%).

At year 10, the statewide employment gap is most pronounced: 24.6% of PSEO participants are in sustained statewide employment compared to 16.7% of non-participants — a difference of nearly eight percentage points. Sustained regional employment is lower among PSEO participants (18.6% vs. 23.9%), and no MN employment record rates are nearly identical (39.8% vs. 40.8%). The year 10 profile of PSEO participants among non-college completers closely mirrors the academic pathway pattern identified in the full population analysis — greater statewide labor market mobility and reduced regional attachment — even among those who ultimately did not complete a postsecondary credential.

Overall, PSEO participation is among the stronger predictors in the high school accomplishments group at year 1, but its association attenuates substantially once postsecondary attendance equalizes. Its most durable finding is a persistent tendency toward statewide rather than regional employment among PSEO participants who did not complete a credential, suggesting that the academic orientation associated with PSEO shapes labor market geography even in the absence of credential completion.

AP exam participation shows a statistically significant association with workforce participation outcomes at all three time points, with Cramer’s V declining substantially over time (V=0.149 at year 1, V=0.101 at year 5, V=0.065 at year 10). Overall, AP exam participation shows a pattern nearly identical in character to ACT participation and PSEO participation: higher postsecondary attendance early, substantially lower regional employment across all time points, and persistently higher statewide employment among those who do enter the labor market. The year 10 attenuation of the overall effect size reflects convergence in no MN employment record rates and non-sustained workforce participation, while the regional versus statewide employment divide remains the most durable signature of AP participation among non-college completers.

Overall, CTE achievement is among the stronger and most interpretable predictors of workforce participation in this population. Total CTE courses taken shows a statistically significant association with workforce participation outcomes at all three time points, with among the highest F-statistics of any variable in the high school accomplishments group (F=146.8 at year 1, F=101.3 at year 5, F=62.1 at year 10). Non-sustained workers show 0.82 fewer courses than regional workers, and the no MN employment record group remains the furthest below (−1.60 courses). Postsecondary attendees and the no MN record group are statistically indistinguishable from each other at year 5, both showing very low CTE course volumes.

Sustained regional employment maintains the highest mean CTE courses, with sustained statewide employment (−0.90 courses) and non-sustained workforce participation (−0.86 courses) both significantly below and statistically indistinguishable from each other — forming a middle tier. This creates a clear three- tier structure at year 10 that mirrors the CTE achievement categorical findings: regional employment at the top, statewide employment and non-sustained workers in the middle, and the no MN employment record group at the bottom.

Overall, total CTE courses taken produces the strongest F-statistics of any variable in the high school accomplishments group and shows the most consistent and durable advantage for sustained regional employment across all three time points. Its pattern directly complements CTE achievement: both variables point to CTE engagement as a robust and growing predictor of regional labor market attachment among non-college completers, with the no MN employment record group consistently showing the lowest CTE engagement at every observation window.

Postsecondary attendance shows by far the strongest bivariate association with workforce participation outcomes of any variable examined in this analysis, with a Cramer’s V of 0.640 at year 1 — well into strong effect territory and more than double the next strongest variable (ACT participation at V=0.302). The association declines substantially over time (V=0.275 at year 5, V=0.124 at year 10) as the structural constraint driving the year 1 relationship — that postsecondary attendees are by definition in the attending ps category — gradually dissolves. The sample sizes are fixed across all three time points (12,102 never attended; 8,693 attended but did not complete), reflecting that attended.ps is a fixed characteristic rather than a time-varying one.

The year 1 association is largely mechanical in nature. Individuals who attended postsecondary account for the entire attending ps category (52.3% of postsecondary attendees are still attending at year 1), while those who never attended postsecondary have zero representation there by definition. Among those already in the labor market at year 1, postsecondary attendees show notably lower sustained regional employment (15.9% vs. 21.8%), lower sustained statewide employment (5.3% vs. 7.0%), but dramatically lower no MN employment record rates (7.1% vs. 31.6%) and lower non-sustained workforce participation (19.5% vs. 39.6%). This suggests that postsecondary attendees who have exited to the labor market by year 1 are more engaged in formal Minnesota employment than non-attendees, even if not yet at sustained thresholds.

The year 5 table uses a different category structure than years 1 and 10, appearing to reflect an intermediate classification scheme. Reading the available categories, postsecondary attendees show higher meaningful regional employment (20.6% vs. 17.8%) and substantially higher meaningful statewide employment (14.4% vs. 8.8%) compared to non-attendees. Non-attendees show higher rates in the top combined outcome category (31.9% vs. 18.0%) and lower no MN employment record rates (24.8% vs. 19.9%), though the non-standard category labels at year 5 limit direct comparability with years 1 and 10.

At year 10, the association has weakened considerably (V=0.124) but remains meaningful. Postsecondary attendees show higher sustained regional employment (12.0% vs. 9.4%) and notably higher sustained statewide employment (10.1% vs. 5.8%), consistent with the academic pathway pattern where postsecondary exposure — even without completion — is associated with greater out-of-region labor market mobility. Non-attendees show lower no MN employment record rates (15.2% vs. 18.0%), suggesting they are somewhat more likely to remain in Minnesota, though in less stable employment. The largest category at year 10 for both groups is the combined outcome category, with non-attendees showing a substantially higher rate (62.4% vs. 50.8%), reflecting their higher overall Minnesota labor market engagement over the decade.

Overall, postsecondary attendance is the strongest single predictor of workforce participation outcomes at year 1 by a wide margin, though much of that association is structural rather than substantive. Its most durable and interpretable finding — consistent with the academic pathway — is that attending postsecondary without completing is associated with higher sustained statewide employment and lower regional employment relative to never attending, a pattern that persists meaningfully through year 10 and mirrors the trajectory observed for ACT participation, AP exams, and PSEO participation among this non-completer population.

Summary: Bivariate Analysis of Workforce Participation Outcomes Among Non-College Completers

The bivariate analysis examined associations between 21 independent variables and workforce participation outcomes at years 1, 5, and 10 post-graduation among Southwest Minnesota high school graduates who did not complete a postsecondary credential. Variables are ranked separately for categorical predictors (Cramer’s V) and continuous predictors (F-statistic from ANOVA) given the absence of a common effect size metric across the two approaches. Rankings reflect general patterns across all three time points, with particular attention to consistency and magnitude of effect.

Categorical Predictors Ranked by Effect Size (Cramer’s V)

1. Postsecondary Attendance (V=0.640, 0.275, 0.124) is the strongest categorical predictor by a substantial margin, though the year 1 association is largely structural — driven by the mechanical relationship between attending postsecondary and the attending ps outcome category. Its most substantive and durable finding is that postsecondary attendees show persistently higher sustained statewide employment and lower regional employment relative to non-attendees across all three time points, consistent with the academic pathway.

2. ACT Participation (V=0.302, 0.155, 0.108) is the strongest high school accomplishment predictor and the strongest variable with a clearly substantive rather than structural association at year 1. Its signature — higher postsecondary attendance, lower regional employment, and substantially higher statewide employment — is the clearest expression of the academic pathway among non-college completers and remains the second strongest categorical predictor at every time point.

3. AP Exam Participation (V=0.149, 0.101, 0.065) ranks third, producing a pattern nearly identical to ACT participation in character — high postsecondary attendance early, persistently lower regional and higher statewide employment — but with smaller effect sizes throughout and greater attenuation by year 10.

4. PSEO Participation (V=0.186, 0.072, 0.082) ranks fourth overall, with a strong year 1 association driven by an 18-percentage-point postsecondary attendance gap that dissipates rapidly. Its residual finding — a persistent tendency toward statewide rather than regional employment among PSEO participants who did not complete — mirrors the broader academic pathway pattern but at modest effect sizes at years 5 and 10. Note that this variable is restricted to 2011 cohorts and later.

5. Special Education Status (V=0.171, 0.107, 0.093) is the strongest demographic predictor, distinguished from other demographic variables by its persistent association with employment instability and reduced statewide labor market mobility across all three time points. Its primary signature is not reduced regional employment but rather elevated non-sustained workforce participation and lower statewide employment that endure through year 10.

6. English Learner Status (V=0.140, 0.074, 0.070) ranks sixth, with a strong year 1 association reflecting lower postsecondary attendance and a persistent and growing absence from Minnesota employment records that is its most distinctive feature — differentiating it from other demographic variables and suggesting out-of-state employment, informal work, or geographic departure as plausible explanations.

7. Economic Status (V=0.137, 0.083, 0.091) ranks seventh, with a strong year 1 association again driven largely by differential postsecondary attendance. Its most durable finding is modestly higher sustained statewide employment among non-disadvantaged graduates and greater employment instability among disadvantaged graduates, though the association weakens considerably from year 1 to year 5.

8. CTE Achievement (V=0.094, 0.106, 0.115) is notable as the only accomplishments variable whose effect size grows at every time point, and the strongest categorical predictor oriented toward regional rather than statewide employment. CTE concentrators and completers show progressively higher regional employment and lower no MN employment record rates relative to the no CTE group, with a 15-percentage-point regional employment gap emerging by year 10.

9. Non-English Home Language (V=0.114, 0.068, 0.061) ranks ninth, closely mirroring the English learner pattern in both direction and character but with consistently smaller effect sizes, suggesting that English learner status captures the relevant variation more precisely. Its independent contribution will require evaluation in multivariate models.

10. EDR (V=0.093, 0.103, 0.107) is the strongest high school characteristics predictor, with a growing association driven primarily by a widening gap in sustained statewide employment between EDR 6E (Southwest Central) and the other two EDRs — a geographic labor market differentiation that is largely invisible in the regional employment category, which is similar across all three EDRs.

11. Gender (V=0.058, 0.087, 0.107) ranks eleventh and is distinctive as the only variable whose effect size increases consistently across all three time points. Its growing association reflects a widening gap in sustained regional employment favoring males and an expanding share of females carrying no MN employment record by year 10, suggesting diverging long-term labor market trajectories by gender.

12. Race/Ethnicity (V=0.069, 0.043, 0.054) shows the weakest and least consistent effect sizes of the categorical variables, with American Indian graduates showing a persistently adverse profile and Asian/Pacific Islander graduates displaying a distinctive pattern of high MN record absence alongside relatively high statewide employment among those who remain. Overall effect sizes are small at all three time points.

13. Community Demographic Description (V=0.054, 0.078, 0.094) shows statistically significant but practically weak associations, with the most interpretable pattern being a growing statewide employment advantage for urban/town/rural mix graduates and higher MN record absence among entirely rural graduates. Effect sizes remain too small to add meaningful explanatory value beyond other variables.

Continuous Predictors Ranked by Effect Size (F-statistic)

1. Total CTE Courses Taken (F=146.8, 101.3, 62.1) produces the highest F-statistics of any continuous variable at all three time points. Its pattern is the mirror image of the academic achievement variables: higher CTE course volume is strongly and consistently associated with sustained regional employment, while the no MN employment record group shows persistently the lowest CTE course-taking at every observation window. The three-tier structure at year 10 — regional employment highest, statewide and non-sustained in the middle, no MN record lowest — is among the clearest patterns in the entire analysis.

2. MCA Reading Score (F=144.3, 42.0, 28.0) is the strongest academic achievement measure, consistent with its role as the primary achievement indicator in the full population multivariate models. Its gradient is clear and durable across all three time points: higher scores are associated with statewide employment and — increasingly over time — no MN employment record, while regional employment and non-sustained work form the lowest-scoring tier. F-statistics are the highest of the three MCA measures at years 1 and 5.

3. ACT Composite Score (F=114.2, 44.6, 18.3) ranks third among continuous predictors, producing a pattern consistent with MCA Reading: higher scores associated with postsecondary attendance early and statewide employment later, with regional employment consistently at the lowest-scoring tier. The score gap between sustained statewide and sustained regional employment grows from 0.79 points at year 1 to 1.43 points at year 10, indicating increasing score-based stratification across the observation windows. Note this variable is restricted to ACT takers only.

4. MCA Math Score (F=89.4, 36.0, 34.6) closely mirrors MCA Reading in pattern but with somewhat lower F-statistics at years 1 and 5. Its year 10 F-statistic (34.6) exceeds MCA Reading (28.0), suggesting math achievement carries slightly stronger differentiation at the later time point. The three-tier consolidation at year 10 — statewide and no MN record as a higher-achieving group, regional and non-sustained as a lower-achieving group — replicates the MCA Reading structure.

5. MCA Science Score (F=84.4, 38.1, 25.1) replicates the MCA Reading and Math patterns with high fidelity across all three time points, including the three-tier consolidation at year 10. As in the full population analysis, its voluntary and self-selected nature limits its utility in multivariate models, but its bivariate associations confirm the broader academic achievement gradient and are consistent with retaining MCA Reading as the primary academic achievement indicator.

6. Average County Unemployment Rate (F=31.3, 44.5, 54.7) is notable as the only continuous variable whose F-statistic increases substantially over time, reflecting a growing labor market geography effect. Its most consistent finding is that graduates achieving sustained statewide employment disproportionately originate from higher-unemployment counties — consistent with a labor market push dynamic — while the no MN employment record group shows unemployment rate profiles indistinguishable from sustained regional workers, suggesting out-of-state departure rather than intra-state mobility.

7. Average County Wages as Percent of State Average (F=35.3, 24.0, 26.1) is statistically significant at all three time points but shows very small absolute differences — all contrasts within one to two percentage points of the state average. Its only durable finding mirrors the unemployment rate variable: graduates in sustained statewide employment consistently originate from lower-wage counties. The negligible absolute magnitude of differences limits its practical significance and supports its exclusion from multivariate models.

Cross-Cutting Observations

Several themes emerge from the full set of bivariate findings. First, the two-pathway structure identified in the full population analysis is fully present among non-college completers: academic indicators (ACT, AP, PSEO, MCA scores, postsecondary attendance) consistently predict statewide labor market mobility, while CTE indicators (CTE achievement, total CTE courses) predict regional attachment — regardless of whether a credential was earned. Second, the no MN employment record category behaves distinctively across the accomplishment variables, increasingly resembling the statewide employment group on academic achievement measures by years 5 and 10, suggesting that a meaningful share of higher-achieving non-completers have left Minnesota rather than failing to engage with its labor market. Third, demographic variables show weaker associations than accomplishment variables across the board, indicating that what students did in high school is more predictive of workforce participation trajectories than who they are. Finally, several variables — community demographic description, county wages, non-English home language — show statistically significant but practically weak associations unlikely to add explanatory value beyond stronger correlated predictors in multivariate models, consistent with decisions made in the full population analysis.


What the Data Tell Us: Workforce Participation Among Non-College Completers

For Southwest Minnesota high school graduates who never earned a postsecondary credential, the years following graduation tell two very different stories depending on the path they took through high school.

The students who were headed somewhere else

Some graduates who ultimately did not complete a postsecondary credential were nonetheless oriented toward college from the start. They took the ACT, participated in AP courses and PSEO, earned higher MCA scores, and went on to attempt postsecondary education even if they didn’t finish it. What happened to them in the labor market looks a lot like what happened to students who did complete degrees — just a dimmer version of the same pattern. They were more likely to leave Southwest Minnesota for employment elsewhere in the state, and by year 10, a striking share of them had simply disappeared from Minnesota employment records entirely. They weren’t failing to find work. The most likely explanation is that they left the state.

This group illustrates something important: academic orientation shapes where people end up even when formal credentials don’t follow. The habits of mind, the networks, the postsecondary experience itself — even an incomplete one — appear to pull people toward broader labor markets and away from the region.

The students who were built for this place

The other story belongs to graduates with deep CTE engagement. Higher numbers of CTE courses taken, CTE concentrator or completer status — these are the students who showed up in regional employment at year 1 and stayed there. By year 10, CTE concentrators were nearly twice as likely to be in sustained regional employment as graduates with no CTE engagement at all. This isn’t a small finding. It suggests that CTE programs are doing exactly what they are supposed to do for students who engage with them seriously: connecting young people to the regional economy and equipping them to stay.

The students who fell through the cracks

The most troubling pattern in the data belongs to graduates with no CTE engagement and no academic orientation — the students who neither pursued a college track nor developed occupational skills through CTE. By year 10, more than half of graduates with no CTE participation carried no Minnesota employment record at all. This group was already the most likely to have no MN employment record at year 1, and that gap only widened over time. Whether they left the state, work in informal economies, or have simply disengaged from the formal labor market, the data cannot say — but their persistent absence from employment records across a decade is the most concerning finding in the entire analysis.

The role of identity and circumstance

Demographic characteristics — race, gender, economic status, special education status, English learner status — do matter, but they matter less than what students did in high school. Special education students show persistent employment instability. English learners and graduates from non-English speaking homes show elevated absence from Minnesota employment records that grows over time, possibly reflecting employment in sectors that don’t generate wage records or communities that maintain strong ties outside Minnesota. Males are more likely to be in sustained regional employment by year 10; females are increasingly likely to carry no MN employment record. These patterns deserve attention, but they operate in the background of the stronger story told by high school experiences.

The geography of opportunity

Where a student went to high school also matters, though modestly. Graduates from EDR 6E — the Southwest Central region anchored by Marshall — show substantially higher rates of employment outside the region compared to graduates from the Upper Minnesota Valley or Southwest EDRs. Graduates from higher- unemployment counties are more likely to leave the region for work elsewhere in the state, consistent with what we would expect when local job markets are tight. But sustained regional employment rates are remarkably similar across the three EDRs, suggesting that the regional economy absorbs similar shares of non-completers regardless of sub-region — it’s the path out of the region that differs.

The bottom line

A decade after high school graduation, the non-college completer population has sorted into roughly three groups: those doing steady work in the region, those doing steady work elsewhere, and a large and growing group with no formal Minnesota employment footprint. CTE engagement is the strongest predictor of being in the first group. Academic orientation — even without a credential — predicts the second. And the absence of either, combined with demographic disadvantage, is the clearest signal of the third.

The regional economy depends heavily on workers who choose to stay. This analysis suggests that CTE programs are the most powerful mechanism available for cultivating that attachment among students who will not complete four-year degrees. At the same time, the growing share of non-completers absent from Minnesota employment records by year 10 — many of them higher-achieving graduates who attended postsecondary without finishing — points to an ongoing and unresolved challenge: how to keep talent in a region when the pathways that develop it often point toward somewhere else.
