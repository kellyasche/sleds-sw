Analysis - attended.ps

data <- master.original %>%
  filter(ps.grad != "Attending ps") %>%
  mutate(attended.ps = ifelse(ps.grad == "Y", "Attended - grad",
                              ifelse(attended.ps == "Yes" & ps.grad == "No", "Attended - did not grad", "Did not attend PS")))

Summary

Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined associations between independent variables and postsecondary attendance outcomes (Did not attend PS, Attended - did not grad, Attended - grad) for 47,189 individuals. Variables are presented in two separate ranked lists by effect size measure.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Cramer’s V

  1. took.ACT (Cramer’s V=0.443) — The strongest categorical predictor. ACT takers show a graduation rate of 67.3% compared to 26.6% for non-takers, with 53.6% of non-takers not attending postsecondary at all.

  2. pseo.participant (Cramer’s V=0.401) — A strong predictor, noting the near-tautological nature of the relationship. PSEO participants show a graduation rate of 76.3% compared to 36.5% for non-participants, with 42.1% of non-participants not attending postsecondary.

  3. economic.status (Cramer’s V=0.299) — Joint strongest demographic predictor alongside special education status. Economically disadvantaged students show a graduation rate of 34.6% compared to 64.4% for non-disadvantaged students, with 42.4% not attending postsecondary.

  4. special.ed.status (Cramer’s V=0.299) — Joint strongest demographic predictor. Special education students show a graduation rate of 21.7% compared to 58.3% for non-special education students, with 59.6% not attending postsecondary.

  5. ap.exam (Cramer’s V=0.230) — A moderate association. AP exam participants show the highest graduation rate of any categorical subgroup examined (79.4%) and the lowest rate of not attending (7.0%).

  6. english.learner (Cramer’s V=0.224) — A moderate association. English learners show a graduation rate of 24.0% compared to 56.3%, with 57.0% not attending postsecondary.

  7. non.english.home (Cramer’s V=0.168) — A weak to moderate association closely mirroring english.learner. Students from non-English speaking homes show a graduation rate of 28.9% compared to 55.7%, with 48.5% not attending.

  8. race.ethnicity (Cramer’s V=0.148) — A weak to moderate association. AI students show the highest rate of not attending (48.6%) while White students show the lowest (22.1%). Asian/PI and Black students show relatively higher graduation rates (42.9% and 39.5% respectively).

  9. gender (Cramer’s V=0.129) — A weak association. Females show a notably higher graduation rate (59.6% vs 47.0%) and lower rate of not attending (23.2% vs 32.7%).

  10. dem.desc (Cramer’s V=0.053) — A very weak association. Entirely rural students show the highest graduation rate (60.9%) and lowest rate of not attending (21.5%).

  11. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.041) — A very weak and inverse association. Higher CTE engagement is modestly associated with lower postsecondary graduation rates, consistent with the workforce pathway sorting mechanism.

  12. edr (Cramer’s V=0.023) — A negligible association. Geographic subregion within Southwest Minnesota has virtually no bearing on postsecondary attendance outcomes.

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

  1. MCA Reading (η²=0.106) — The strongest continuous predictor with a medium effect. Graduates score significantly higher than non-completers and did not attend PS individuals score significantly lower than both attended groups, with a gap of 0.721 between graduates and non-attendees.

  2. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.094) — A small to medium effect, restricted to ACT takers. The gap between graduates and did not attend PS individuals is very large (estimate=-3.883), reflecting the powerful sorting role of academic achievement in postsecondary pathway selection.

  3. MCA Science (η²=0.088) — A small to medium effect, noting the voluntary and self-selected nature of participation. Pattern mirrors Reading and Math with graduates scoring significantly higher than all other groups.

  4. MCA Math (η²=0.079) — A small to medium effect closely mirroring MCA Reading and Science. Graduates score significantly higher than non-completers and did not attend PS individuals score significantly lower than both attended groups.

  5. avg.unemployment.rate (η²=0.007) — A negligible effect consistent with the opportunity cost hypothesis. Did not attend PS individuals experienced significantly lower average unemployment rates, suggesting favorable local labor market conditions are associated with direct workforce entry.

  6. total.cte.courses.taken (η²=0.008) — A negligible effect. Graduates show significantly fewer CTE courses than non-completers and did not attend PS individuals, further reinforcing the inverse relationship between CTE engagement and postsecondary attendance.

  7. avg.wages.pct.state (η²=0.006) — A negligible effect. Local wage levels explain less than one percent of variance in postsecondary attendance outcomes.

Overall Pattern High school accomplishment variables — particularly took.ACT, pseo.participant, and MCA scores — are the strongest predictors of postsecondary attendance outcomes, considerably stronger than demographic or geographic variables. Among demographic variables, economic status and special education status are the joint strongest predictors, with both showing that disadvantaged groups are primarily distinguished by much higher rates of not attending rather than higher rates of attending without completing, suggesting access rather than completion is the primary barrier. A consistent inverse relationship between CTE engagement and postsecondary attendance across both CTE achievement and total CTE courses taken, combined with negligible geographic effects, reinforces the pathway sorting mechanism in which CTE engagement is associated with direct regional workforce entry rather than postsecondary pursuit. Local labor market conditions show negligible associations, suggesting that individual academic preparation and demographic characteristics are far more powerful determinants of postsecondary attendance than local economic conditions in this regional context.

Summary

Overview Cross-tabulation analysis examined the relationship between demographic variables and postsecondary attendance outcomes (Did not attend PS, Attended - did not grad, Attended - grad) for 47,189 individuals. Variables are ranked by Cramer’s V from strongest to weakest association.

Ranked by Cramer’s V:

1. Economic Status (Cramer’s V=0.299) The strongest demographic predictor of postsecondary attendance. Non-disadvantaged students show a graduation rate of 64.4% compared to just 34.6% for economically disadvantaged students, while 42.4% of economically disadvantaged students did not attend postsecondary compared to 19.4% of non-disadvantaged students. Economic disadvantage is clearly a substantial barrier to both accessing and completing postsecondary education.

2. Special Education Status (Cramer’s V=0.299) Equally strong as economic status. Special education students show a dramatically lower graduation rate (21.7% vs 58.3%) and a strikingly high rate of not attending postsecondary (59.6% vs 22.9%). Notably, the attended but did not graduate rates are virtually identical between the two groups (18.8% vs 18.8%), suggesting that when special education students do attend they face similar completion challenges to non-special education students but are far less likely to attempt postsecondary in the first place.

3. English Learner Status (Cramer’s V=0.224) A moderate association. English learners show a much lower graduation rate (24.0% vs 56.3%) and a much higher rate of not attending (57.0% vs 24.9%), while attended but did not graduate rates are nearly identical between the two groups (19.0% vs 18.8%), mirroring the pattern observed for special education status.

4. Non-English Home Language (Cramer’s V=0.168) A weak to moderate association closely mirroring the English learner pattern. Students from non-English speaking homes show lower graduation rates (28.9% vs 55.7%) and higher rates of not attending (48.5% vs 25.9%).

5. Gender (Cramer’s V=0.129) A weak association. Females show a notably higher graduation rate (59.6% vs 47.0%) and lower rate of not attending (23.2% vs 32.7%), consistent with well-established national patterns of female postsecondary attainment advantage. The gender gap in graduation rates is notably larger in this dataset than typically observed nationally.

6. Race/Ethnicity (Cramer’s V=0.148) A weak to moderate association with notable variation across groups. AI students show the highest rate of not attending (48.6%) while White students show the lowest (22.1%). Asian/PI and Black students show relatively higher graduation rates (42.9% and 39.5% respectively) compared to AI (24.7%) and Hispanic (26.5%) students. Small sample sizes for minority groups warrant cautious interpretation.

Summary Economic status and special education status are the joint strongest demographic predictors of postsecondary attendance outcomes (both Cramer’s V=0.299), with both variables showing large differences in graduation rates and not attending rates. A consistent and important pattern across economic status, special education, English learner, and non-English home language variables is that disadvantaged groups are primarily distinguished by much higher rates of not attending rather than higher rates of attending without graduating — the attended but did not graduate rates are nearly identical across all disadvantaged and non-disadvantaged groups — suggesting that access rather than completion is the primary barrier for these groups. Gender shows a weak but substantively meaningful association with females showing considerably higher graduation rates than males.

Summary

Overview Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined the relationship between high school characteristics and local labor market variables and postsecondary attendance outcomes (Did not attend PS, Attended - did not grad, Attended - grad) for 47,189 individuals. Categorical variables are ranked by Cramer’s V and continuous variables by eta-squared.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Cramer’s V

1. Community Demographic Description (Cramer’s V=0.053) A very weak but statistically significant association. Entirely rural students show the highest graduation rate (60.9%) and the lowest rate of not attending (21.5%), while town/rural mix students show the lowest graduation rate (51.2%) and the highest rate of not attending (29.8%). Urban/town/rural mix students fall in between (55.1% graduated, 26.0% did not attend). Attended but did not graduate rates are nearly identical across all three community types (17-19%), suggesting that community type influences the decision to attend rather than completion once enrolled.

2. EDR (Cramer’s V=0.023) A negligible association. Graduation rates are nearly identical across EDR 6E Southwest Central (51.5%), EDR 6W Upper Minnesota Valley (52.9%), and EDR 8 Southwest (54.5%), as are rates of not attending (29.2%, 29.1%, and 26.7% respectively). Geographic subregion within Southwest Minnesota has virtually no bearing on postsecondary attendance outcomes.

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

1. Average Unemployment Rate (η²=0.007) A negligible effect size despite statistical significance. Tukey HSD post-hoc tests reveal that did not attend PS individuals experienced significantly lower average unemployment rates than both attended groups (estimate= -0.303 vs did not grad, p<0.001; estimate=-0.218 vs grad, p<0.001), consistent with the opportunity cost hypothesis — favorable local labor market conditions are associated with direct workforce entry rather than postsecondary attendance. The difference between the two attended groups is smaller but significant (estimate=-0.085, p<0.001).

2. Average Wages as Percentage of State Average (η²=0.006) A negligible effect size. Tukey HSD post-hoc tests show that graduates experience slightly higher relative wages than non-completers (estimate=0.004, p<0.001), while did not attend PS individuals experience significantly lower relative wages than both attended groups, consistent with the opportunity cost hypothesis but with a very small practical effect.

Summary High school characteristics and labor market variables show negligible to very weak associations with postsecondary attendance outcomes. EDR shows a negligible association, confirming that geographic subregion within Southwest Minnesota has virtually no bearing on postsecondary attendance decisions. Community demographic description shows a slightly stronger but still very weak association, with entirely rural students showing the highest graduation rates. Both labor market variables show associations consistent with the opportunity cost hypothesis but with negligible effect sizes, suggesting that local labor market conditions play a very modest role in postsecondary attendance decisions in this regional context.

High School Accomplishments

Summary

Overview Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined the relationship between high school accomplishment variables and postsecondary graduation (College completer vs Non-college completer) for 47,189 individuals, with the exception of pseo.participant which excludes the 2008-2010 graduation cohorts. Binary categorical variables report phi (φ) and multi-category variables report Cramer’s V. Continuous variables are ranked by eta-squared.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Effect Size

1. took.ACT (φ=0.389) The joint strongest categorical predictor alongside pseo.participant. ACT takers show a completion rate of 67.3% compared to just 26.6% for non-ACT takers — a gap of over 40 percentage points, the largest differential observed across any categorical variable for postsecondary graduation. Non-ACT takers show a non-completion rate of 73.4%, suggesting that not taking the ACT is a very strong signal of postsecondary non-completion.

2. pseo.participant (φ=0.389) Equally strong as took.ACT. PSEO participants show a completion rate of 76.3% compared to 36.5% for non-participants — a gap of nearly 40 percentage points. Given the near-tautological nature of this relationship, the association should be interpreted with the understanding that PSEO participants are already in a postsecondary pathway while still in high school.

3. ap.exam (φ=0.220) A moderate association. AP exam participants show a completion rate of 79.4% — the highest of any categorical subgroup examined — compared to 48.6% for non-AP exam students, a gap of nearly 31 percentage points.

4. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.052) A very weak association with an interesting inverse pattern. No CTE students show the highest completion rate (56.4%), followed by CTE Participants (55.2%) and CTE Concentrators or Completors (50.6%). The differences are modest but consistent with the broader pathway sorting mechanism — higher CTE engagement is modestly associated with lower postsecondary completion rates, likely reflecting the direct workforce entry pathway associated with intensive CTE engagement.

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

1. MCA Reading (η²=0.087) The strongest continuous predictor with a small to medium effect. College completers score significantly higher than non-completers (estimate=0.555, p<0.001), confirming that Reading achievement is the strongest MCA predictor of postsecondary graduation.

2. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.082) A small to medium effect restricted to ACT takers. College completers show substantially higher ACT scores than non-completers (estimate=2.847, p<0.001), reflecting the well-established relationship between academic achievement and postsecondary completion.

3. MCA Science (η²=0.078) A small to medium effect, noting the voluntary and self-selected nature of Science MCA participation. College completers score significantly higher than non-completers (estimate=0.501, p<0.001).

4. MCA Math (η²=0.073) A small to medium effect closely mirroring MCA Reading and Science. College completers score significantly higher than non-completers (estimate=0.563, p<0.001).

5. Total CTE Courses Taken (η²=0.008) A negligible effect size despite statistical significance. Non-completers show significantly more CTE courses than completers (estimate=0.703, p<0.001), consistent with the inverse relationship between CTE engagement and postsecondary completion observed throughout this analysis.

Summary High school accomplishment variables show strong to moderate associations with postsecondary graduation outcomes, with took.ACT and pseo.participant tied as the strongest categorical predictors (both φ=0.389) and MCA Reading emerging as the strongest continuous predictor (η²=0.087). The patterns closely mirror those observed for postsecondary attendance, with academic achievement and college preparedness indicators strongly associated with completion and CTE engagement showing a modest inverse relationship. The negligible effect of total CTE courses taken on graduation, combined with its meaningful association with regional workforce participation, continues to reinforce the complementary nature of academic and CTE pathways in Southwest Minnesota.

Summary

Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined associations between independent variables and postsecondary graduation institution sector (Public 4-year, Public 2-year, Private not-for-profit 4-year, Other college type) among the 24,998 college completers in the dataset. Variables are presented in two separate ranked lists by effect size measure. All categorical variables report Cramer’s V.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Cramer’s V

  1. took.ACT (Cramer’s V=0.381) — The strongest categorical predictor. Non-ACT completers are overwhelmingly concentrated in public 2-year institutions (75.2%), while ACT takers show much higher rates of public 4-year (46.1%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (15.2%).

  2. pseo.participant (Cramer’s V=0.322) — The second strongest categorical predictor. PSEO participants show notably higher rates of public 4-year (49.2%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (15.7%), while non-participants are heavily concentrated in public 2-year institutions (55.4%).

  3. ap.exam (Cramer’s V=0.290) — A moderate to strong association. AP exam participants show high rates of public 4-year (53.0%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (24.6% — the highest of any categorical subgroup), while non-AP exam completers are concentrated in public 2-year institutions (42.8%).

  4. special.ed.status (Cramer’s V=0.221) — The strongest demographic predictor. Special education completers are overwhelmingly concentrated in public 2-year institutions (78.6%) with virtually no representation in private not-for-profit 4-year institutions (2.4%).

  5. economic.status (Cramer’s V=0.195) — The second strongest demographic predictor. Economically disadvantaged completers show much higher public 2-year rates (51.7% vs 30.9%) and lower public 4-year rates (29.0% vs 44.0%).

  6. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.167) — A moderate association with a clear dose-response pattern. CTE Concentrators or Completors show the highest public 2-year rate (46.8%) while No CTE completers show the highest private not-for-profit 4-year (20.3%) and public 4-year rates (47.0%).

  7. english.learner (Cramer’s V=0.155) — A weak to moderate association. English learner completers show a strikingly high public 2-year rate (69.8%) and very low private not-for-profit 4-year rate (3.9%).

  8. gender (Cramer’s V=0.151) — A weak to moderate association. Males show considerably higher public 2-year rates (42.7% vs 30.4%) while females show higher rates across all 4-year institution types.

  9. non.english.home (Cramer’s V=0.124) — A weak association mirroring english.learner. Students from non-English speaking homes show high public 2-year rates (60.3%) and low private not-for-profit 4-year rates (6.3%).

  10. race.ethnicity (Cramer’s V=0.069) — A very weak association. Hispanic completers show the highest public 2-year rate (57.6%) while White completers show the highest public 4-year rate (43.0%).

  11. edr (Cramer’s V=0.079) — A very weak association. EDR 6E Southwest Central completers show the highest private not-for-profit 4-year rate (17.1%) while EDR 6W Upper Minnesota Valley shows the highest public 2-year rate (43.1%).

  12. dem.desc (Cramer’s V=0.057) — A very weak association. Urban/town/rural mix completers show the highest public 4-year rate (45.0%) while entirely rural completers show the highest public 2-year rate (39.8%).

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

  1. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.199) — A large effect size — the largest observed for any continuous variable across the entire postsecondary pathway analysis. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score substantially higher than all other groups, with a gap of 5.411 points between private not-for-profit 4-year and public 2-year graduates — the largest single group difference observed for any continuous variable in this analysis.

  2. MCA Reading (η²=0.130) — A medium to large effect. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score highest while public 2-year graduates score the lowest, with a gap of 0.650 between these groups.

  3. MCA Science (η²=0.122) — A medium effect, noting the voluntary nature of participation. The ordering across institution types mirrors Reading and ACT composite throughout.

  4. Total CTE Courses Taken (η²=0.096) — A small to medium effect with the most distinctive pattern — public 2-year graduates show significantly more CTE courses than all other groups (estimate=3.065 vs private not-for-profit 4-year), confirming the CTE-to-community-college pathway at the institutional level.

  5. MCA Math (η²=0.092) — A small to medium effect mirroring Reading and Science. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score highest and public 2-year graduates score the lowest.

  6. avg.unemployment.rate (η²=0.008) — A negligible effect. Public 2-year graduates experienced significantly lower unemployment rates than other institution type graduates.

  7. avg.wages.pct.state (η²=0.002) — A negligible effect. Local wage levels explain virtually no variance in institution type among completers.

Overall Pattern The predictors of institution sector among college completers show the strongest associations observed across the entire postsecondary pathway analysis, with ACT composite score producing the largest effect size of any continuous variable examined (η²=0.199). The institutional sorting by academic achievement and CTE engagement is remarkably clear and consistent across every variable group examined — high academic achievers graduate from private not-for-profit and public 4-year institutions, while lower academic achievers and higher CTE engagers who do complete postsecondary do so almost exclusively at public 2-year institutions. Demographic disadvantage variables show strong concentrations in public 2-year institutions across special education status, economic status, English learner status, and non-English home language. Geographic variables remain very weak, confirming that individual-level characteristics overwhelmingly determine institution type among college completers in Southwest Minnesota. The two-pathway structure — academic to 4-year and CTE to community college — that has been a consistent theme throughout this analysis is confirmed most definitively by this institution sector outcome.

Both labor market variables show negligible effect sizes, confirming that local economic conditions play virtually no practical role in determining institution type among those who complete postsecondary education. The very weak geographic associations suggest that individual-level characteristics are far more important determinants of institution type than community of origin within Southwest Minnesota.

High School Accomplishments

Summary

Overview Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined the relationship between high school accomplishment variables and postsecondary graduation institution sector (Public 4-year, Public 2-year, Private not-for-profit 4-year, Other college type) among the 24,998 college completers in the dataset, with the exception of pseo.participant which excludes the 2008-2010 graduation cohorts. This outcome shows some of the strongest associations observed across the entire postsecondary pathway analysis.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Effect Size

1. took.ACT (Cramer’s V=0.381) The strongest categorical predictor of institution sector. ACT takers show dramatically higher rates of public 4-year (46.1% vs 12.5%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (15.2% vs 3.7%), while non-ACT completers are overwhelmingly concentrated in public 2-year institutions (75.2% vs 27.8%). This is the clearest institutional sorting pattern observed across any variable — non-ACT completers who do finish postsecondary almost exclusively do so at community colleges.

2. pseo.participant (Cramer’s V=0.322) The second strongest categorical predictor. PSEO participants show a notably higher rate of public 4-year graduation (49.2% vs 27.8%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (15.7% vs 8.3%), while non-participants are more heavily concentrated in public 2-year institutions (55.4% vs 24.2%). PSEO participation is clearly associated with 4-year institutional pathways even among those who ultimately complete a credential.

3. ap.exam (Cramer’s V=0.290) A moderate to strong association. AP exam participants show high rates of public 4-year (53.0%) and private not-for-profit 4-year graduation (24.6%), while non-AP exam completers are more concentrated in public 2-year institutions (42.8% vs 12.3%). The private not-for-profit 4-year rate for AP exam participants (24.6%) is the highest observed for any categorical subgroup on this variable.

4. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.167) A moderate association with a clear dose-response pattern. CTE Concentrators or Completors show the highest public 2-year graduation rate (46.8%) and the lowest private not-for-profit 4-year rate (9.4%), while No CTE completers show the highest private not-for-profit 4-year rate (20.3%) and public 4-year rate (47.0%). The inverse relationship between CTE engagement and 4-year institution graduation is the clearest evidence yet of the CTE-to-community-college pathway in Southwest Minnesota.

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

1. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.199) A large effect size — the largest observed for any continuous variable across all outcomes examined in this entire analysis. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score substantially higher than all other groups (estimate=2.603 vs other college type, p<0.001), while public 2-year graduates score the lowest (estimate=-5.411 vs private not-for-profit 4-year, p<0.001). The gap between private not-for-profit 4-year and public 2-year graduates (5.411 points) is the largest single group difference observed across any continuous variable for any outcome in this analysis.

2. MCA Reading (η²=0.130) A medium to large effect. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score significantly higher than all other groups (estimate=0.244 vs other college type, p<0.001), while public 2-year graduates score the lowest (estimate=-0.650 vs private not-for-profit 4-year, p<0.001). Public 4-year graduates score significantly higher than public 2-year graduates (estimate=0.580, p<0.001).

3. MCA Science (η²=0.122) A medium effect, noting the voluntary nature of participation. The ordering across institution types mirrors Reading and ACT composite, with private not-for-profit 4-year graduates scoring highest (estimate=0.397 vs other college type, p<0.001) and public 2-year graduates scoring the lowest (estimate=-0.674 vs private not-for-profit 4-year, p<0.001).

4. Total CTE Courses Taken (η²=0.096) A small to medium effect with the most distinctive pattern of any continuous variable for this outcome — the direction reverses relative to academic achievement variables. Public 2-year graduates show significantly more CTE courses than all other groups (estimate=1.984 vs other college type, p<0.001; estimate=3.065 vs private not-for-profit 4-year, p<0.001), while private not-for-profit 4-year graduates show significantly fewer CTE courses than all other groups. This confirms the CTE-to-community-college pathway at the institutional level.

5. MCA Math (η²=0.092) A small to medium effect closely mirroring MCA Reading and Science. Private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score highest (estimate=0.358 vs other college type, p<0.001) and public 2-year graduates score the lowest (estimate=-0.665 vs private not-for-profit 4-year, p<0.001).

Summary High school accomplishment variables show the strongest associations with institution sector of any variable group examined for this outcome, with ACT composite score producing a large effect size (η²=0.199) — the largest observed for any continuous variable across the entire postsecondary pathway analysis. The institutional sorting by academic achievement is remarkably clear and consistent: high academic achievers graduate from private not-for-profit and public 4-year institutions, while lower academic achievers who do complete postsecondary do so almost exclusively at public 2-year institutions. CTE engagement shows the complementary pattern — higher CTE engagement is strongly associated with public 2-year graduation, with total CTE courses taken producing one of the largest group differences observed for any continuous variable in this analysis. Together these findings provide compelling and definitive evidence of two distinct postsecondary pathways in Southwest Minnesota — an academic pathway leading to 4-year institutions and a CTE pathway leading to community colleges — with high school achievement variables serving as the primary sorting mechanism between them.

Summary

Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined associations between independent variables and postsecondary graduation location (PS grad region, PS grad MN, PS grad outside MN) among the 24,998 college completers in the dataset. Variables are presented in two separate ranked lists by effect size measure. All categorical variables report Cramer’s V.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Cramer’s V

  1. EDR (Cramer’s V=0.179) — The strongest predictor overall and by far the strongest geographic predictor observed across the entire postsecondary pathway analysis. EDR 8 Southwest completers show the highest out-of-state graduation rate (40.8%), EDR 6W Upper Minnesota Valley show the highest in-Minnesota rate (46.9%), and EDR 6E Southwest Central show the highest regional graduation rate (36.0%).

  2. took.ACT (Cramer’s V=0.164) — The strongest categorical high school accomplishment predictor. Non-ACT completers show the highest regional graduation rate (46.8%) while ACT takers show considerably higher out-of-state rates (35.3%) and lower regional rates (27.4%).

  3. non.english.home (Cramer’s V=0.159) — The strongest demographic predictor. Students from non-English speaking homes show a strikingly high regional graduation rate (61.3%) and very low out-of-state rate (11.7%).

  4. english.learner (Cramer’s V=0.154) — A weak to moderate association closely mirroring non.english.home. English learners show the highest regional graduation rate of any demographic subgroup (63.2%) and the lowest out-of-state rate (12.0%).

  5. economic.status (Cramer’s V=0.145) — A weak to moderate association. Economically disadvantaged completers show higher regional graduation rates (42.0% vs 27.1%) and lower out-of-state rates (24.6% vs 35.6%).

  6. ap.exam (Cramer’s V=0.128) — A weak association. AP exam participants show notably higher in-Minnesota rates (45.4%) and lower regional rates (20.5%), with similar out-of-state rates to non-AP exam completers.

  7. pseo.participant (Cramer’s V=0.116) — A weak association. Non-participants show higher regional graduation rates (36.7% vs 26.0%) while PSEO participants gravitate toward non-regional institutions.

  8. race.ethnicity (Cramer’s V=0.101) — A weak association. Hispanic completers show the highest regional graduation rate (57.2%) while White completers show the lowest (28.5%) and the highest out-of-state rate (33.9%).

  9. special.ed.status (Cramer’s V=0.101) — A weak association. Special education completers show a higher regional graduation rate (49.6% vs 29.6%) and lower out-of-state rate (21.9% vs 33.6%).

  10. gender (Cramer’s V=0.054) — The weakest demographic predictor. Females show a slightly higher regional rate (32.4% vs 28.8%) while males show a slightly higher out-of-state rate (35.6% vs 30.7%).

  11. dem.desc (Cramer’s V=0.049) — A negligible association. Entirely rural completers show the highest out-of-state rate (40.0%) and lowest regional rate (27.6%).

  12. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.048) — A very weak association. CTE Concentrators or Completors show the highest regional graduation rate of any CTE group (32.9%) while No CTE completers show the lowest (26.2%) and the highest in-Minnesota rate (40.4%).

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

  1. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.048) — A small effect. PS grad region completers score significantly lower than both PS grad MN (estimate=-2.238) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate=-2.145), while MN and out-of-state graduates show no significant difference from each other.

  2. MCA Science (η²=0.044) — A small effect mirroring ACT composite. Regional completers score significantly lower than both other groups across all comparisons.

  3. MCA Math (η²=0.034) — A small effect with the same ordering. Regional completers score significantly lower than MN (estimate=-0.299) and out-of-state graduates (estimate=-0.423).

  4. MCA Reading (η²=0.031) — A small effect. Regional completers score significantly lower than both MN (estimate=-0.270) and out-of-state graduates (estimate=-0.319).

  5. avg.unemployment.rate (η²=0.020) — A small effect — the largest for any continuous labor market variable across any postsecondary outcome. PS grad outside MN completers experienced significantly lower unemployment rates than both PS grad MN and PS grad region completers, suggesting greater economic resources and mobility among out-of-state graduates.

  6. Total CTE Courses Taken (η²=0.008) — A negligible effect. Regional completers show significantly more CTE courses than MN and out-of-state graduates, consistent with the CTE regional retention theme.

  7. avg.wages.pct.state (η²=0.004) — A negligible effect. PS grad outside MN completers experienced slightly higher relative wages, but local wage levels explain virtually no variance in graduation location.

Overall Pattern This outcome shows the most complex predictor structure of any postsecondary outcome examined, with geographic, demographic, and high school accomplishment variables all contributing meaningfully. Three clear patterns emerge. First, EDR is the strongest single predictor, revealing meaningful within-region variation in where completers graduate — a finding unique to this outcome and with direct policy relevance for regional planning. Second, demographic disadvantage variables — non-English home language, English learner status, economic disadvantage, and special education status — are all associated with higher regional graduation rates, reflecting the role of community ties, geographic constraints, and concentration in regional public 2-year institutions among these groups. Third, academic achievement and college preparedness variables are consistently associated with non-regional graduation, while CTE engagement is associated with regional graduation — extending the two-pathway structure identified throughout this analysis all the way through to graduation location. Together these findings confirm that postsecondary graduation location is shaped by a combination of where students come from, who they are, and what kind of academic or CTE pathway they pursued in high school.

High School Accomplishments

Summary

Overview Cross-tabulation analysis and one-way ANOVA examined the relationship between high school accomplishment variables and postsecondary graduation location (PS grad region, PS grad MN, PS grad outside MN) among the 24,998 college completers in the dataset, with the exception of pseo.participant which excludes the 2008-2010 graduation cohorts. Effect sizes are generally weaker for this outcome than for other postsecondary outcomes, reflecting that graduation location is shaped by many factors beyond academic preparation alone.

Categorical Variables - Ranked by Effect Size

1. took.ACT (Cramer’s V=0.164) The strongest categorical predictor of graduation location. Non-ACT completers show the highest regional graduation rate (46.8%) and the lowest out-of-state graduation rate (21.6%), while ACT takers show considerably lower regional graduation rates (27.4%) and higher out-of-state rates (35.3%). ACT participation is associated with greater geographic mobility — completers who took the ACT are considerably more likely to graduate from institutions outside Southwest Minnesota.

2. ap.exam (Cramer’s V=0.128) A weak association. AP exam participants show notably higher in-Minnesota graduation rates (45.4% vs 33.7%) and lower regional graduation rates (20.5% vs 33.7%) compared to non-AP exam completers. Out-of-state graduation rates are similar between the two groups (34.2% vs 32.5%), suggesting AP exam participation is associated with mobility within Minnesota rather than out of state specifically.

3. pseo.participant (Cramer’s V=0.116) A weak association. Non-PSEO participants show a higher regional graduation rate (36.7% vs 26.0%) while PSEO participants show higher MN and out-of-state graduation rates. PSEO participation is associated with completing postsecondary at non-regional institutions, consistent with the finding that PSEO is strongly associated with 4-year institutional pathways which are less concentrated in the Southwest Minnesota region.

4. cte.achievement (Cramer’s V=0.048) A very weak association. Reading across rows, CTE Concentrators or Completors show the highest regional graduation rate (32.9%) of any CTE group, while No CTE completers show the lowest regional graduation rate (26.2%) and the highest in-Minnesota graduation rate (40.4%). CTE Participants fall in between (30.8% regional, 37.6% MN). This pattern is consistent with the broader CTE regional retention theme throughout this analysis — higher CTE engagement is associated with staying in the region, including for postsecondary completion. Conversely, No CTE completers show the strongest tendency toward non-regional Minnesota institutions, consistent with their higher rates of 4-year non-regional institution graduation identified in the institution sector analysis.

Continuous Variables - Ranked by Eta-Squared

1. ACT Composite Score (η²=0.048) A small effect. Tukey HSD post-hoc tests reveal a distinctive pattern — PS grad region completers score significantly lower than both PS grad MN (estimate= -2.238, p<0.001) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate=-2.145, p<0.001), while PS grad MN and PS grad outside MN graduates show no significant difference from each other (p=0.391). Academic achievement distinguishes regional from non-regional graduates but does not differentiate between MN and out-of-state graduates.

2. MCA Science (η²=0.044) A small effect mirroring ACT composite. PS grad region completers score significantly lower than PS grad MN (estimate=-0.318, p<0.001) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate=-0.409, p<0.001), while out-of-state graduates score slightly higher than MN graduates (estimate=0.091, p<0.001).

3. MCA Math (η²=0.034) A small effect. PS grad region completers score significantly lower than PS grad MN (estimate=-0.299, p<0.001) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate= -0.423, p<0.001), while out-of-state graduates score slightly higher than MN graduates (estimate=0.124, p<0.001).

4. MCA Reading (η²=0.031) A small effect with the same ordering across groups. PS grad region completers score significantly lower than PS grad MN (estimate=-0.270, p<0.001) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate=-0.319, p<0.001).

5. Total CTE Courses Taken (η²=0.008) A negligible effect. PS grad region completers show significantly more CTE courses than PS grad MN (estimate=0.755, p<0.001) and PS grad outside MN graduates (estimate=0.679, p<0.001), while MN and out-of-state graduates show no significant difference (p=0.381). Regional graduation is associated with higher CTE course-taking, consistent with the cte.achievement pattern above.

Summary High school accomplishment variables show weak to very weak associations with graduation location among college completers, with effect sizes considerably smaller than for other postsecondary outcomes. The most important and consistent finding across both categorical and continuous variables is a clear regional versus non-regional divide — academic achievement variables consistently distinguish regional completers from all others, with regional completers scoring lower and showing lower rates of academic preparedness indicators, while CTE engagement is consistently associated with regional graduation. ACT participation, PSEO, and AP exam are all associated with non-regional graduation, while CTE Concentrators or Completors show the highest regional graduation rate of any CTE group. These patterns confirm that the two-pathway structure — academic to non-regional institutions and CTE to regional institutions — extends all the way through to where individuals ultimately graduate.

Overall Summary of Postsecondary Pathway Analysis

This analysis examined what predicts postsecondary attendance and graduation for all 47,189 individuals in the dataset, and then what predicts credential level, institution type, and graduation location among the 24,998 who did complete a postsecondary credential. Together these outcomes map the full postsecondary sorting process for Southwest Minnesota high school graduates.

Who Goes to College and Who Completes

The data paint a clear and consistent portrait of who does and does not pursue and complete postsecondary education in Southwest Minnesota. Postsecondary attendance and graduation are strongly predicted by the same cluster of individual characteristics, and the gaps between groups are large.

Students who are most likely to attend and complete postsecondary education are those who took the ACT (67.3% completion rate vs 26.6% for non-ACT takers), participated in PSEO (76.3% vs 36.5%), took AP exams (79.4% vs 48.6%), and scored higher on MCA Reading and Math. These students are also more likely to be female, non-special education, and not economically disadvantaged. In short, students who are academically prepared and college-oriented in high school are dramatically more likely to complete a postsecondary credential.

Students who are least likely to attend and complete are those who did not take the ACT (53.6% did not attend at all), are identified as special education (59.6% did not attend, and of those who did attend only 21.7% graduated), are economically disadvantaged (42.4% did not attend, and only 34.6% of those who did attend graduated), are English learners (57.0% did not attend), or come from non-English speaking homes (48.5% did not attend). American Indian and Hispanic students also show notably lower attendance and graduation rates than their White, Asian/PI, and Black peers. Males are less likely than females to attend and complete at every stage.

A critically important finding is that for most disadvantaged groups, the primary barrier is access rather than completion. Across special education status, economic status, English learner status, and non-English home language, the rate of attending but not completing postsecondary is nearly identical between disadvantaged and non-disadvantaged groups. What differs dramatically is whether students attempt postsecondary at all. This suggests that interventions focused on encouraging postsecondary attendance among disadvantaged groups may have greater impact than interventions focused on improving completion rates.

CTE engagement shows a consistent and modest inverse relationship with postsecondary attendance and graduation. CTE Concentrators or Completors show lower postsecondary graduation rates (50.6%) than CTE Participants (55.2%) or No CTE students (56.4%), and take significantly more CTE courses than those who go on to graduate. This is not evidence of a negative outcome — it reflects the sorting of CTE-engaged students into direct workforce entry rather than postsecondary pathways, consistent with the regional workforce retention findings throughout this analysis.

Geographic and local labor market variables show virtually no association with who attends or completes postsecondary education. Whether a student comes from EDR 6E, 6W, or 8, or from an entirely rural, town/rural mix, or urban/town/ rural mix community, has negligible bearing on their postsecondary pathway. Individual-level characteristics overwhelmingly determine who goes to college and who completes, not where in Southwest Minnesota they grew up. Local unemployment rates show a small association consistent with the opportunity cost hypothesis — students from lower unemployment areas are slightly more likely to enter the workforce directly — but the effect is negligible in practical terms.

Where College Completers Go and What They Earn

Among the 24,998 individuals who did complete a postsecondary credential, a second and equally clear sorting process determines what kind of credential they earn, what type of institution they graduate from, and where that institution is located.

The portrait of a bachelor degree or higher completer at a 4-year institution outside the Southwest Minnesota region is quite specific: they took the ACT (ACT takers show a 67.6% bachelor degree or higher rate vs 21.0% for non-ACT takers among completers), participated in PSEO (71.4% vs 40.4%), took AP exams (83.5% vs 52.6%), scored substantially higher on all MCA subjects and on the ACT composite (private not-for-profit 4-year graduates score 5.4 ACT points higher than public 2-year graduates on average), are female, non-special education, and not economically disadvantaged. They are also more likely to have graduated from ACT-oriented high school programs with minimal CTE course-taking. They graduate from institutions outside Southwest Minnesota — either elsewhere in Minnesota or out of state — and as the bivariate workforce participation analysis showed, they are the group most likely to be working outside the region or outside Minnesota entirely a decade after graduation.

The portrait of a sub-baccalaureate completer at a public 2-year institution within the Southwest Minnesota region is equally specific: they did not take the ACT (75.2% of non-ACT completers graduate from public 2-year institutions), are CTE Concentrators or Completors (46.8% public 2-year graduation rate vs 20.6% for No CTE completers), took significantly more CTE courses, scored lower on MCA subjects, are more likely to be male, more likely to be a special education student (78.6% of special education completers graduate from public 2-year institutions), more likely to be economically disadvantaged (51.7% public 2-year rate vs 30.9%), and more likely to be an English learner (69.8% public 2-year rate). They graduate from institutions within the Southwest Minnesota region, and as the workforce participation analysis showed, they are the group most likely to be working in the region a decade after graduation.

Geography matters more at this stage than for attendance and graduation. EDR of origin is the strongest single predictor of graduation location (Cramer’s V=0.179), with EDR 8 Southwest completers showing the highest out-of-state graduation rate (40.8%) and EDR 6E Southwest Central completers showing the strongest regional retention (36.0% regional graduation). This is the only stage of the postsecondary pathway where geographic origin within Southwest Minnesota meaningfully shapes outcomes, and it has direct implications for subregional workforce planning.

The Two Pathways

Taken together, the postsecondary pathway analysis reveals two distinct and consistently divergent pathways through which Southwest Minnesota high school graduates move into the workforce.

The academic pathway is characterized by ACT participation, AP exam, PSEO, high MCA scores, non-special education status, economic advantage, and female gender. It leads through postsecondary attendance and graduation to bachelor degree or higher credentials at public or private 4-year institutions outside the Southwest Minnesota region and ultimately to employment outside the region or outside Minnesota entirely.

The CTE pathway is characterized by CTE engagement, lower academic achievement indicators, special education status, economic disadvantage, English learner or non-English home language status, and male gender. It leads to either direct workforce entry without postsecondary or to sub-baccalaureate credentials at regional public 2-year institutions and ultimately to employment within the Southwest Minnesota region.

These two pathways are not hierarchical in value — they serve fundamentally different workforce functions and both are essential to the Southwest Minnesota regional economy. But they are strikingly distinct in who follows them and where they lead, and understanding them as parallel systems rather than a single ladder is essential to designing effective regional education and workforce development policy.
